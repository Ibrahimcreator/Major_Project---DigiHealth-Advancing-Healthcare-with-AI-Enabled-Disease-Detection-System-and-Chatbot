DigiHealth - Diabetes Prediction

About Diabetes

Diabetes is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by high blood sugar levels resulting from either insufficient insulin production or the body's ineffective use of insulin.

Symptoms

  • Increased thirst (polydipsia): Feeling unusually thirsty and needing to drink more fluids.
  • Frequent urination (polyuria): Urinating more often than usual, particularly at night.
  • Increased hunger (polyphagia): Feeling hungry more frequently, even after eating.
  • Fatigue: Feeling tired and lacking energy, even with adequate rest.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Losing weight without trying, often despite increased appetite.

Causes

  • Type 1 diabetes: Autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, leading to little or no insulin production.
  • Type 2 diabetes: Insulin resistance, where cells fail to respond effectively to insulin, combined with inadequate insulin secretion.
  • Gestational diabetes: High blood sugar levels that develop during pregnancy, often due to hormonal changes and insulin resistance.

Treatment Options

  • Lifestyle modifications: Healthy diet, regular exercise, weight management, and smoking cessation.
  • Medications: Oral medications or insulin therapy to lower blood sugar levels and manage diabetes complications.
  • Blood sugar monitoring: Regular monitoring of blood glucose levels to assess treatment effectiveness and make adjustments as needed.

Complications

  • Cardiovascular disease: Diabetes increases the risk of heart disease, stroke, and peripheral artery disease.
  • Nerve damage (neuropathy): High blood sugar levels can damage nerves throughout the body, leading to pain, numbness, or tingling, particularly in the extremities.
  • Kidney damage (nephropathy): Diabetes can cause kidney damage or failure over time, leading to protein in the urine and reduced kidney function.
  • Eye damage (retinopathy): Diabetes can damage the blood vessels in the retina, leading to vision problems or blindness if left untreated.
  • Foot problems: Diabetes increases the risk of foot ulcers, infections, and poor circulation, which can lead to serious complications and even amputation if not properly managed.
